Does National Diet Predict Disease Mortality Rates?

by Hansy Piou and Ben Zheng

Overview

Introduction and Motivation

We've all heard of the saying "You are what you eat" but how well does this adage hold up against cold hard facts (and data)? Though widely discussed in an informal context, there are few long-term studies showing how diet in uences well-being. Our project brought a data science approach to this important issue, focused on using countries' historical food consumption data to predict diabetes and cardiovascular disease mortality rate for that country. Using a wide range of countries, food groups and years of data collection to build our models, we hoped to find results supporting the notion that past diet a ects present health on a national level.

Objective

For this project we wanted to see if national consumption habits had an influence on disease mortality rates, and if so, how great an influence. In addition, we wanted to explore which food groups had more significant effects.

Approach

We began our study by reading in agriculture and livestock consumption data, filling in missing values by using a 3-nearest geographical neighbors approach. We then created a baseline model, fitting a linear regression model to 2000 diabetes/heart disease data and 1999 consumption data to ensure that the consumption proceeds the deaths and then testing the model on 2012 disease data and 2011 consumption data. Our advanced model explored all possible ranges of years from when we first started collecting data on currently extant countries to 2000, selecting the best range of years. We then selected a subset of predictors using both forward selection and LASSO regularization, resulting in 2 models for a given disease dataset. Finally, we compared the forward selection and LASSO models with the baseline model on both diabetes and heart disease data.
