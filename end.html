Conclusions and Future Work

Regardless of whether LASSO or forward selection was used, our advanced models had R^2s of ~0.4, meaning that diet is an important factor in diabetes/heart disease mortality rate, but not an overwhelming one. We suspect that lifestyle, environment, income, and access to healthcare are all just as if, if not more important factors.

Nonetheless, although the two models reported different top 10 predictors, some trends were common among both models, and even both diseases. Sugars, and foods which are generally high in carbohydrates, are confirmed to have a significant - and through background knowledge, known to be negative - effect on oneâ€™s health.

We would like to extend our work to show how changes in food consumption rates affect changes in disease mortality rates in countries. Since body weight is more closely tied to diet than mortality rate, we can extend this year-subset model to Obesity/Overweight. Additionally, while it would be more computationally intensive, we can try ranking the features across all possible year intervals, find the best subset of features, and then use it to find the best year interval.
